BLACK HOLES

What is a black hole?

Black holes are an extremely rare phenomenom. Their mass is extremely big so, according to gravitational formulas (Force=g*mass), their gravity is also extremely high. So to sum up, a black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, including light, has enough energy to escape it. How black holes are born is still not very clear, but the theory of general relativity states that a sufficiently big mass within a sufficiently small space could form a blackhole. This could be possible since Albert Einstein proposed that masses deformed the spacetime. So according to this, a sufficiently big mass can curve space and affect how we perceive time.

How can we see blackholes?

So we have stated that not even light can escape black holes. In fact, nothing can once it has crossed the event horizon or the "limit of no return". Once something goes past this point, the gravity is so strong that not even light can escape it. So with this clear you may be wondering, how can we see black holes if not even light can escape from them? So that's a very logical question. In fact, we actually can't see black holes. That's why they are called black holes, because they are black. We aknowledge their pressence thanks to the deformation of space they cause around them. Light and other objects such as stars just start to curve when passing nearby a black hole. Black holes also expell radiation, which many times is visible due to the extremely high temperatures, and create visible rings of orbitting photons and other particles which can also reveal the pressence of a black hole.

Parts of a black hole

So a black hole has several parts.
The first one is the acretion disc. This isa disc of superheated gas and dust that whirls around a black hole at extreme speeds producing electromagnetic radiation that reveal the black hole's location, as we've already mentioned. The next element is the innermost stable orbit which is basically the inner edge of the acretion disc, and is also the last point in which matter can orbit the black hole wothout falling past the point of no return. We can also find the relativistic jets. Basically, when a black hole absorbs matter such as stars or other objects it expells jets of particles and blasts out radiation out from the black hole's poles at near light speed. This jets can extend for thousands of light-years into space. Going deeper into the black hole we find the photon sphere. This sphere is created by photons generated by other phenomenoms of the black hole such as relativistic jets. Gravity just outside the event horizon is strong enough to bend photons' paths so we see a bright ring surrounding a circular black shadow. Next up is the event horizon. As we have already explained, the event horizon is the radius where matter and energy cannot escape the black hole's gravity, the point of no return. Reaching the center of the black hole we find the singularity. At this point at the core of the black hole, matter has colapsed into a region of near infinite density called singularity. All the matter and energy that fall into the black hole end up here.

There are supermassive black holes at the center of some galaxies such as our galaxy, the Milky Way. The black hole at the center aof our galaxy is called Sagittarius A.

Black holes are extremely rare and still unknown phenomenoms. Science is far from near to understanding them. There are still many questions that we cannot answer yet. What would happen if you fell into a black hole? Would time stop due to the near infinite gravity in the core of a black hole? What happens to the matter that is swallowed by the black hole? With our current technology we just can observe their behaviour in space.
